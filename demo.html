Practice Plan

Tuesday, March 25, 2026

What We Worked On and Why

Today we focused on resonance balancing across the passaggio in "Dove sono." The core discovery: when you let the vowel narrow slightly on the ascending line, the warmth stays without losing brightness. You do not have to choose one or the other. The key is listening for both qualities at the same time and trusting the vowel modification to do the work.

We also spent time on "Ain't It a Pretty Night," where the same principle applies in the chest-to-mix transition. The buzziness you are hearing on the D4 phrase is a good sign. It means the resonance is engaged. We just need to smooth the transition so it does not arrive suddenly.

Exercises for This Week

1. Descending five-note scale on [u] to [o] (warm-up)

What it sounds like: Start on a comfortable pitch on "oo," open to "oh" on the way down. Move up by half steps.

What it is for: Establishes warmth as the baseline resonance quality. The [u] keeps the vocal tract long and the larynx easy.

What to listen for: Warmth through the whole scale. If the sound gets thin at the bottom, the jaw may be dropping too far. Keep the vowel space inside, not outside.

2. Ascending arpeggio on [i] with slight [e] modification above E5

What it sounds like: "ee" on a 1-3-5-8 pattern. Above E5, let the vowel drift toward "eh" without losing the forward placement.

What it is for: Builds brightness through the passaggio without pressing. The [i] keeps the resonance focused. The modification above E5 prevents the squeeze that happens when you try to maintain pure [i] too high.

What to listen for: Brightness that feels easy, not pushed. If you hear a hard edge or feel tension at the top, the modification is not happening soon enough. Start the vowel change a half step earlier.

3. Messa di voce on a sustained A4

What it sounds like: Start soft, swell to full, return to soft on a single pitch. Use [a].

What it is for: Teaches you to maintain resonance balance across dynamic changes. This is the skill that makes "Dove sono" work: the line swells and recedes, and the voice needs to stay balanced through all of it.

What to listen for: Consistent warmth through the swell. The brightness will increase naturally as you get louder. That is fine. What you do not want is the warmth disappearing when you get loud. If the sound gets brittle at forte, back off the volume and focus on keeping the space open.

4. Octave slide on [a] with rib monitoring

What it sounds like: Slide from a comfortable pitch up an octave on "ah." Hands on ribs.

What it is for: Connects breath support to the passaggio crossing. Your pattern has been to let the ribs collapse right at the transition point, which starves the upper notes of air.

What to listen for: Evenness. The slide should feel like one gesture, not two notes with a bump in the middle. If you feel a bump, the ribs probably dropped. Check with your hands.

Repertoire Work

Dove sono i bei momenti

Your target sound: Warm and round on the sustained lines, with brightness arriving naturally at the climaxes. Not the other way around.

  • Practice the recitative section first, speaking the Italian in rhythm. Feel the character's questions before singing them.
  • On the ascending phrase at "che il mio cor," use the [i]-to-[e] modification from exercise 2. The vowel change should be invisible to a listener.
  • Run the aria once per practice session for continuity. Mark the moments where you feel warmth and brightness coexisting. Those are your anchors.

Ain't It a Pretty Night

Current phase: Characterization Integration

  • Focus on the D4 transition phrase this week. The buzziness you are hearing is resonance engaging. Let it be there.
  • Speak Susannah's text as a monologue before singing. She is dreaming out loud. The vocal quality should reflect wonder, not effort.
  • Work the final page slowly, connecting the breath support from exercise 4 to the high phrase.

Practice Structure

Pre-recital hearing is April 18. Three weeks of focused work.

  • Warm up: 10 minutes. Descending [u]-[o] scales, then the [i] arpeggio.
  • Exercises: Messa di voce + octave slides, 10 minutes. Focus on consistency, not power.
  • Rep work: 25-30 minutes. Alternate between "Dove sono" and "Ain't It a Pretty Night." Spend at least one session this week on your other recital pieces.
  • Cool down: Descending lip trills, gentle humming on [m].
  • Stop when fatigue shows up. If the brightness disappears and everything sounds dull no matter what you do, you are tired. Rest.

Tuesday, March 25, 2026 · 60 min

What We Worked On

  • Dove sono i bei momenti (Mozart): Ascending line at "che il mio cor," passaggio balancing, recitative phrasing
  • Ain't It a Pretty Night (Floyd): D4 chest-to-mix transition, final page high phrase

Exercises:

  • Descending five-note [u] to [o]: establishing warmth as baseline resonance
  • Ascending arpeggio on [i] with modification above E5: building brightness through the passaggio without pressing
  • Messa di voce on A4: maintaining resonance balance across dynamic changes
  • Octave slide on [a] with rib monitoring: connecting breath support to passaggio crossing

Listening targets: warmth on sustained lines, brightness arriving naturally at climaxes, buzziness at D4 as a sign of resonance engagement

What Shifted

The passaggio crossing in "Dove sono" landed three times cleanly when you let the [i] vowel narrow toward [e] on the ascent. Your reaction was, "Oh, I do not have to push it up there." Exactly. The resonance does the work when you give it the right vowel shape.

In "Ain't It a Pretty Night," the D4 transition smoothed out when you stopped trying to eliminate the buzziness and instead let it be part of the sound. That was a significant perceptual shift: hearing resonance engagement as a feature, not a problem.

Where You Are

Passaggio balancing is in the associative phase: you can find it when you think about the vowel modification, and it is starting to happen on its own in familiar passages. The breath support coordination is further along. You maintain rib expansion through most phrases without prompting now. The next threshold is doing both at once under performance conditions, which is what the hearing will test.

What's Next

Continue the [i]-to-[e] modification work in "Dove sono," especially on the ascending phrases in the B section. Start running the full recitative + aria as a unit to build stamina and dramatic continuity. In "Ain't It a Pretty Night," let the buzziness at D4 settle in. Do not try to fix it. It is correct.

What Shifted

The recitative in "Dove sono" came alive when you started speaking the Italian as a real question rather than a musical phrase. The vocal quality changed immediately: more warmth, more presence, less mechanical. That connection between intention and sound is exactly what we are building toward for the recital.

On "Someone to Watch Over Me," you found a vocal color shift in the bridge that was genuinely different from the verse. Softer, more intimate, with a quality that suits the microphone. That was new.

Where You Are

Italian diction is solid and moving toward automatic. The German [y] vowel still needs conscious attention, especially when it sits above the staff. The character connection in the recitative is in the cognitive phase: you can find it, but it takes deliberate focus. With repetition this week, it should start to feel more natural.

What's Next

Memorize the recitative section of "Dove sono" this week so the text can live in your body, not on the page. Continue the vocal color exploration in "Someone to Watch Over Me" for studio class on Friday. For "Widmung," focus on the [y] vowel in context: speak the text slowly, then sing, keeping the vowel rounded and warm.
